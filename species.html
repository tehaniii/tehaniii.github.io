SEA TURTLE SPECIES

A large diverse population of sea turtles, including the hawksbill, loggerhead, leatherback, green, and olive ridley types, may be found in Sri Lanka. These amazing animals breed on the sand beaches of the nation and help maintain  biological harmony of marine environments. Sea turtles in Sri Lanka are an essential component of the country's coastal biodiversity due to their distinctive traits and essential role within the ocean.




Hawksbill Turtle

Tropical waterways across the world are home to the critically endangered Hawksbill Turtle. They are mostly located in Sri Lanka's southern coast, and they are well-known for having exquisite shells that are highly valued for their ornamental qualities. Due to its critical contribution to the preservation of coral reef health, the Hawksbill Turtle is a crucial species in Sri Lanka's ecosystem. The maturity age for these turtles range from 20-25 years. Beaches with high population densities are Hikkaduwa Beach, Weligama Beach, and Koggala Beach.




Olive Ridley Turtle

The Indian and Pacific waters are home to the little sea turtle species known as the Olive Ridley Turtle. One of the most frequent sea turtles in Sri Lanka, it may be seen around the country's coastline. Due to a decreasing population, the Olive Ridley Turtle, which is distinguished by its olive-colored shell, is listed as an endangered species. The maturity age for Olive Ridleys ranges from 10-15 years. Beaches with high population densities of these creatures could be in Rekawa Beach, Induruwa Beach, and Kosgoda Beach.




Loggerhead Turtle

Large sea turtles may be found around the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific oceans. One of these is the loggerhead turtle. Its huge head, which is noticeably bigger than those of other sea turtle species, gave rise to its name. Loggerhead turtles are uncommon in Sri Lanka, and the eastern coast of the island is where they often lay their eggs. The maturity age for Loggerhead turtles range from 25-35 years. Beaches with high population densities are Uppuveli Beach and Nilaveli Beach.




Green Sea Turtle

In warm coastal seas all around the world, the green turtle, a medium-sized sea turtle, may be found. They are regularly seen in Sri Lanka's southern coast, and they are distinguished by their herbivorous diet, which consists primarily of sea grass. Due to habitat damage and hunting, Sri Lanka's Green Turtle population is threatened. The Green Turtle is also considered as an endangered species. The maturity age for these turtles range from 25-50 years. Beaches with high population densities are Unawatuna Beach, Mirissa Beach, and Tangalle Beach.




Leatherback Turtle

The Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific seas all include populations of the leatherback turtle, which is the biggest species of marine turtle. They are regarded as a highly endangered species in Sri Lanka and are rarely observed because of habitat damage, pollution, and illicit poaching. They are the largest of all sea turtles and the largest leatherback ever recorded was 2.56m long and it weighed 916kg. beaches with high population density of these turtles are in Ambalangoda, Yala and Induruwa.

About

The Sri Lanka Turtle Hatchery is a major sanctuary that contributes significantly to the preservation and maintenance of the diversified sea turtle population in the nation. The hatchery tirelessly protects sea turtle nests against predation and theft thanks to its enthusiastic and committed employees and volunteers, guaranteeing the survival of these amazing animals. The hatchery cares for the eggs and monitors hatching, ensuring that the young are released safely into the ocean and helping to protect these magnificent sea turtles and the sensitive marine habitats they call home.
